Life Extension Research

This Fund's Mission

Only 5% of people living today will have no health problems during their lifetime. Humans suffer and die unnecessarily from cancer, Alzheimer's, Parkinson's, cardiac disease, multiple sclerosis, schizophrenia, dementia, and old age.

Life in the 2040s will make our current world seem almost barbaric by comparison; especially in how we diagnose and treat diseases. Today, you might not even know you have a serious disease or receive treatment until you're already showing late-stage symptoms. These labs aim to change that.

Featured Labs

Ganguly Lab

The Ganguly Lab researches how circuits in the brain create complex, coordinated movements. They use artificial neural interfaces to supplement motion.

At a glance

  • 13 PhD students and postdoctoral researchers
  • Dr. Ganguly received the Presidential Early Career Award for Scientists and Engineers, the US' highest honor for early-stage science professionals
    •

    Show More

    Why we chose this lab

    One of the most concerning impacts of strokes and neurodegenerative disease is that it prevents patients from controlling their limbs after nerve damage. The Ganguly Lab does important work to enable patients recoving from CNS damage to maintain control over their bodies.

    Projects From this Lab

    1. When you learn a skill, does the brain update during the actual process of learning or later (during sleep, for example)?

    2. How do scattered networks of neurons in the brain combine to control movement at very specific and well-timed intervals?

    3. Building an artificial feedback system to help stroke patients regain control of movement

    Collaborators:

    The UCSF Center for Neural Engineering and Prostheses

    Professor Ganguly's lab is at the University of California San Francisco, which has over 1,800 active inventions, 5 Nobel Prize winners, and has spawned over 185 startups (including Genentech).

    Professor Ganguly has been working with neural engineering and plasticity since his Ph.D. thesis in 2004.

    Wu Lab

    Most brain diseases damage the part of the neuron that conducts signals - axons. This damage, axonopathy, is often the earliest sign of disease. The Wu Lab investigates how this damage occurs in Alzheimer’s, Huntington’s, Down syndrome, and other diseases.

    At a glance

  • 3 PhD/MDs, and collaboration with 8 faculty members at UCSD and UC Irvine
  • Professor Wu has been working on axon damage for 25 years
    •

    Show More

    Why we chose this lab

    Neurodegenerative disease leaves patients with no good recovery options, and exactly how diseases kill neurons is also unclear. The Wu Lab seeks to uncover the basic principles behind what goes wrong in Alzheimer's or Parkinson's and explore an important avenue for treating damaged neurons.

    Projects From this Lab

    1. In models of Huntington's disease, how does disabling the transport of a protein called BDNF lead to cell death?

    2. Investigating how a protein called Tau damages the transport process along axons in several brain diseases

    3. Figuring out how a specific gene mutation can kill neurons that control your muscles and lead to Charcot-Marie-Tooth disease

    4. Creating a mutant version of a very common brain protein (NGF, or nerve growth factor) in order to treat neurons that are dying

    Collaborators:

    The UCSD School of Medicine

    Professor Wu's lab is at the University of California San Diego, a billion-dollar research enterprise in one of the most densely concentrated medical innovation hubs in the US.

    Professor Wu has been working with axon damage since his Ph.D. thesis in 1994.
